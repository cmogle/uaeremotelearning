## Immediate Support / Common Blockers

Section eyebrow:
Student support

Section title:
What do you need right now?

Section caption:
Choose one common blocker. Read only that panel. Do the steps, then come back if you still need more help.

Support team title:
Need a person, not another checklist?

Support team caption:
These are the fastest routes to real support for students.

### Common blocker 1

Title:
I feel overwhelmed

Steps:
1. Stop looking at everything else for a moment.
2. Take one slow breath in and one slow breath out.
3. Choose one task only.
4. Work for five minutes or ask for help.

Note:
One small step is enough right now.

### Common blocker 2

Title:
I cannot focus

Steps:
1. Put your phone away.
2. Close tabs you do not need.
3. Set a five-minute timer.
4. Look only at the first instruction.

Note:
The goal is not perfect focus. The goal is starting.

### Common blocker 3

Title:
I missed the start of the lesson

Steps:
1. Join or open the lesson now.
2. Read only the latest instruction first.
3. Email your teacher if you are late or lost.
4. Set a reminder for the next lesson.

Note:
Rejoining matters more than catching up perfectly.

### Common blocker 4

Title:
I do not understand the task

Steps:
1. Read the instruction out loud.
2. Underline one word you recognise.
3. Ask what the first step is.
4. Start with one part, not the whole task.

Note:
Understanding often starts after asking one clear question.

### Common blocker 5

Title:
I do not like reading on a screen

Steps:
1. Use text-to-speech or a read-aloud tool.
2. Read one sentence at a time.
3. Cover the rest of the text if that helps.
4. Ask for the task in smaller chunks.

Note:
Listening and chunking are valid ways to access the work.

Extra support:
- Mac shortcut: Command plus F5 for VoiceOver
- Windows shortcut: Control plus Windows key plus Enter for Narrator

### Common blocker 6

Title:
I need an adult to help me

Steps:
1. Subject teacher for classwork or lesson confusion.
2. Form tutor for daily support or if the day is going wrong.
3. Pastoral or wellbeing support if you feel stressed or overwhelmed.
4. Support for Learning if you need help understanding, organising, or getting started.

Note:
You do not have to carry a difficult day on your own.

### Common blocker 7

Title:
I cannot manage my emails

Steps:
1. Check your email at set times, not constantly.
2. Look first for your teacher's name, deadlines, lesson links, or reminder words.
3. Reply straight away if it will take less than two minutes.
4. Star, archive, or close emails once you have dealt with them.

Note:
You do not need to check emails all day.

Extra support:
- Try a two-minute inbox reset: star anything important, reply if it is quick, then archive or close the rest.
- Ask your teacher if you are unsure which emails matter most.

### Common blocker 8

Title:
My eyes hurt

Steps:
1. Look away from the screen for 20 seconds.
2. Lower the screen brightness if it feels too strong.
3. Blink slowly and relax your eyes.
4. Stand up for a moment, then come back to one small task.

Note:
A short screen break can help you reset.

### Common blocker 9

Title:
I cannot sit still

Steps:
1. Stand up and work if that helps.
2. Stretch for 30 seconds.
3. Move for two minutes.
4. Come back and do one task only.

Note:
Movement can help your attention reset.

### Common blocker 10

Title:
I cannot join a lesson

Steps:
1. Email your teacher straight away.
2. Copy in your form tutor or pastoral lead if the issue is affecting the day.
3. Say what is wrong in one sentence.
4. Do not disappear. Let school know you are still here.

Note:
Ask for help as soon as you can.

### Common blocker 11

Title:
I miss my friends

Steps:
1. Message a friend to check in.
2. Arrange a short call or study session if you can.
3. Tell your tutor if isolation is making the day harder.
4. Remember this is temporary and people still want to hear from you.

Note:
Staying connected can make the day feel lighter.

Support phrases:
- I am not sure what the first step is.
- Could you explain that in a smaller chunk?
- I am overwhelmed and need help getting started.
- I cannot join right now but I am still here.
- I am stuck starting.
- Can I talk to someone?

Support contacts:
- Support for Learning: f.nicconmara_jcd@gemsedu.com - for understanding work, organisation, and getting started
- Form tutor: Use the school platform or agreed tutor contact route for daily support
- Subject teacher: Use the live lesson, email, or Google Classroom comments for classwork and lesson help
- Counselling Team: counselling_jcd@gemsedu.com - for stress, overwhelm, or emotional support

## School-Day Expectations

Day title:
What matters in the school day

Lesson title:
How remote lessons usually work

Routine title:
What helps remote learning work

Routine caption:
Keep expectations clear, but keep the pathway calm and achievable.

Phrase title:
Simple words students can use

Phrase caption:
These scripts reduce the pressure of asking for help when a student is stuck or overwhelmed.

Day expectations:
1. Join each lesson or task as close to the start time as you can.
2. Check Google Classroom and your timetable every day.
3. Attend form time and key updates when they are part of the day.
4. Complete work in manageable chunks, not all at once.
5. Ask for help before stress builds up.
6. Use reminders, notes, and routines to stay oriented.

Lesson rules:
1. Your work will usually be on Google Classroom.
2. Teachers may use Google Meet, email, chat, or posted tasks.
3. Read the latest instruction first before looking further back.
4. If you cannot join, contact the teacher and copy in the right pastoral lead.
5. Follow the timetable and use reminders if that helps.

Shared expectations:
1. Join lessons on time where possible.
2. Follow instructions respectfully.
3. Meet deadlines when you can, and communicate early if you cannot.
4. Keep your camera on when a teacher asks and school expectations require it.
5. Be kind to other people and to yourself.

Support bullets:
1. Break tasks into smaller parts before motivation drops.
2. Use the timetable as an orientation tool, not a pressure tool.
3. Prefer clear next steps over long explanations.
4. Make support visible before behaviour becomes the issue.
5. Check emails at set times instead of carrying them all day.

## Adult / Educator Guidance

Adult section label:
Adult guidance

Adult link label:
For adults supporting a learner

Adult section title:
For teachers, tutors, and families

Adult section caption:
Use these blocks when a learner is dysregulated, missing learning, or needs support adapted to additional needs.

Adult summary title:
Use this section after the student has found their immediate next step

Adult summary text:
Keep the student journey primary. Use these adult guidance blocks to reduce barriers, make re-entry feel possible, and adapt support without increasing pressure.

### Adult guidance card 1

Title:
When a learner is overwhelmed

Summary:
Reduce demand before repeating instructions. Regulation comes before compliance.

Steps:
1. Acknowledge the stress without adding urgency.
2. Offer one clear next step, not a full list.
3. Reduce the amount of reading or task volume temporarily.
4. Reconnect the learner with the right adult if the barrier is emotional, not academic.

### Adult guidance card 2

Title:
When a learner keeps missing lessons

Summary:
Treat repeated absence as a support signal as well as an attendance issue.

Steps:
1. Find out whether the barrier is access, anxiety, executive function, or misunderstanding.
2. Send the simplest possible re-entry instruction.
3. Use pastoral and support-for-learning routes early.
4. Make the next lesson feel rejoinable, not punitive.

### Adult guidance card 3

Title:
When additional needs are in play

Summary:
Consistency, chunking, and lower cognitive load often matter more than extra explanation.

Steps:
1. Use shorter instructions with one action per line.
2. Offer read-aloud, reduced text load, or visual chunking.
3. Keep routines predictable and visible.
4. Check whether the learner needs access adjustments rather than more encouragement.

### Adult guidance card 4

Title:
When practical blockers are building up

Summary:
Small barriers like inbox overload, screen fatigue, or restlessness can quietly stop learning before anyone notices.

Steps:
1. Check whether the learner is overwhelmed by emails, tabs, or too many instructions.
2. Offer a short movement or screen break before re-entry.
3. Reduce the task to one visible first action.
4. Keep the message practical and calm so the learner can rejoin quickly.

Accessibility title:
Accessibility and additional needs

Accessibility caption:
Design support around reduced load, clearer sequencing, and flexible ways to access the same learning.

Accessibility features:
1. Chunk the work
   - Break tasks into smaller visible steps and avoid presenting the whole demand at once.
2. Offer multiple access routes
   - Support reading, listening, speaking, and visual structure so the same learning is available in more than one format.
3. Reduce language load
   - Prefer short sentences, concrete verbs, and one instruction at a time.
4. Keep support explicit
   - Show learners exactly who they can contact and what words they can use when they are stuck.
5. Build in physical and visual resets
   - Offer short screen breaks, stretch moments, and one-task re-entry points when attention or regulation is dropping.

Adult footer title:
Reduce the barrier first, then rebuild the routine.

Adult footer text:
Adults can help most by making re-entry feel possible: clarify the next step, lower the language load, and reconnect the learner with the right person early.

Adult footer note:
If concern continues, use tutor, pastoral, and support-for-learning routes before the learner falls further behind.

## Wellbeing and Human Support

Wellbeing title:
Wellbeing and further support

Wellbeing caption:
Keep calm, regulation, and visible human support close by when the learning day starts to feel too big.

Support page title:
If the day is becoming too hard

Support page intro:
This section is for emotional support, regulation, and human contact points.

Helpful habits:
1. Sleep and wake at a consistent time where possible.
2. Drink water and eat before concentration drops.
3. Move between lessons, even for one minute.
4. Build in time away from the screen after study blocks.
5. Stay in touch with friends or trusted adults when remote learning feels isolating.

Reset steps:
1. Slow your breathing down.
2. Relax your shoulders and jaw.
3. Look away from the screen or stretch for a moment.
4. Notice five things you can see.
5. Choose one small next action or one adult to contact.
